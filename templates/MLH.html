Michigan Legal Help

A legal self-help website.

I worked with a team of 4 as a UX researcher for Michigan Legal Help for SI 622: Needs ASsessment & Usability Evaluation. Over the course of the Winter 2016 semester we conducted a usability analysis of their website. Michigan Legal Help is currently using our findings in their website redesign.


Client: Michigan Legal Help

Date: Jan 2016 - April 2016

Team: Erin Lilly, Vishnu Kumaar Mahendran, & Jing Wang

Roles: Project Manager, User Experience Researcher

Tools: Interaction map, interviews, personas & scenarios, comparative analysis, surveys, heuristic evauation, usability testing & user interviews

OUR CLIENT

Michigan Legal Help is an online legal self‐help website, created to help people manage simple civil legal problems without the assistance of a lawyer. The website is most commonly used by those having legal issues concerning family, abuse, or housing. One challenge our client seeks to address is the accessibility of legal resources for low-income users who lack a college education. Michigan Legal Help provides toolkits, forms, and information about community resources.

INTERVIEWS

We began with meeting our client in person to get an idea of their needs, goals, and expectations.
Afterwards, we interviewed seven possible users, three of which were held at the 15th District Court. This interviews gave us an understanding of what users expect in a legal self-help resource, as well as how they currently find legal information. We used these findings to create three personas and scenarios, which we used to empathize with user needs.

COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

With this background, we conducted a comparative analysis to see how MLH stood against its competitors. We based our comparison on 9 important categories: organization, linguistic clarity, accuracy, trustworthiness, flexibility, design, functional content, affordability, and intended users. Our results revealed the strengths and weaknesses of MLH, while giving us ideas of how features can be changed to best fit user needs.

SKETCHING & BRAINSTORMING

Each of us brought 2-3 rough mockups of possible designs for The Origin. From these we picked our favorite designs and began brainstorming ways to implement different actions in the game, such as selecting a player to test, marking suspicions, or sending packages. We left our brainstorming sessions with the following decisions:

To signify the amount of Infected vs Researchers, instead of red or blue dots, use an Infected & Researcher icon with numbers.
Use a grid system.
Mark suspicions on players by tapping.
Make Infection notification dramatic to differentiate it from a clue.
Split screens further into suspicions, actions, messages, and rules.
Use tabs to navigate between screens.
